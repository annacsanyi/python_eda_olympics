Summer Olympics Exploratory Data Analysis Project

1. Introduction

The objective of this exploratory data analysis (EDA) is to uncover trends and patterns in the Summer Olympics between 1976 and 2008. The dataset includes all medal winners from the 1976 Montreal Games to the 2008 Beijing Games, with information such as sport, discipline, event, gender, age, and nationality of athletes.

The analysis will be guided by the following key questions:

  1. How has the total number of medals awarded changed between 1976 and 2008?
  2. How has the number of female medalists evolved over time compared to male medalists?
  3. Which sports are male- or female-majority in terms of athlete participation?
  4. Which countries won the most medals overall, and do host countries tend to win significantly more?
  5. Which sports show the widest distribution of medals across countries, and which are dominated by only a few?

Through answering these questions, the goal is to gain meaningful insights into Olympic participation, performance, and trends across nations, genders, and sports.

As the number of medals in total also increased throughout the year, the number only would be misleading. To avoid this, the share of medals won by the host countries in each year was calculated. This relative variable allows for cleaner comparison. In the final dataset (host_country_data) only the 8 hosting countries were retained to have a cleaner plot.

The interpretation of host-country medal shares must consider historical and dataset factors: the Soviet Union dissolved after 1991, Canada/USA and the Soviet Union missed the 1980 and 1984 Games due to boycotts, and Greece and China only have records from the 1980s onward.

Findings:

  • Soviet Union saw a dramatic host effect, winning about one-third of all medals in 1980.
  • United States also benefited, with a sharp increase in medal share in the 1984 Los Angeles Games.
  • Chinas share rose to ~9% in the 2008 Beijing Games, its highest in the dataset.
  • Spain (Barcelona 1992) and Australia (Sydney 2000) also showed clear boosts.
  • Canada did not see a medal share increase in 1976 but improved in 1984.
  • Greece had only a modest increase in 2004.

Overall, results suggest hosting often correlates with higher medal share, though not uniformly across all nations. Political context (e.g., boycotts in 1980 and 1984) also played a major role.

5. Conclusion

This analysis explored the Summer Olympics between 1976 and 2008 through five guiding questions. Across the dataset, the number of medals awarded steadily increased, reflecting the expansion of Olympic events. A key finding is the substantial rise in female participation: while women represented less than one-third of medalists in 1976, they made up nearly half by 2008, showing the growing inclusivity of the Games. The examination of gender dominance by sport revealed that only a few sports leaned female-majority, whereas male-dominated sports were far more common, though progress was visible by 2008.

Host countries generally benefited from a home advantage, with several nations experiencing notable increases in medal share when hosting. Finally, the analysis of medal distribution by sport showed that some disciplines, like Athletics and Boxing, are widely contested internationally, while others, such as Softball and Baseball, remain concentrated among a handful of nations.

In conclusion, these findings highlight how the Olympics not only reflect athletic achievement but also broader historical, political, and social trends.
